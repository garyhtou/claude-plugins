# Synthesis: the output contract

The deliverable is a **decision-grade, cited answer that shows its work**, not a
summary of what the internet says. This file is the contract the final report must
meet. The fill-in template is in `assets/report-template.md`.

## Principles

- **Lead with the answer.** The reader wants the bottom line and how much to trust it,
  first. Put the overall confidence next to it.
- **Every claim is traceable.** If it is in the report, it has a citation or it is
  explicitly labeled as inference/unknown. No orphan assertions.
- **Verified, contested, and unknown are separated visibly.** The reader must be able
  to tell at a glance what is solid, what is disputed, and what nobody could confirm.
  Collapsing these three into one confident voice is the failure this skill exists to
  prevent.
- **Confidence reflects corroboration, not repetition.** Say why a claim is high or
  low, in terms of independent sources and recency.
- **The unknowns are a feature.** A crisp list of what could not be determined is more
  valuable than a fabricated completeness, because it tells the reader exactly where
  to be careful or dig further.

## Required sections

1. **Bottom line** (2 to 5 sentences). The direct answer to the framed question, with
   an **overall confidence** (high / medium / low) and the one-line reason for it.
2. **Scope and method** (brief). The question as you framed it, which lenses you ran,
   depth level, and how many rounds. This is what lets a reader judge the effort
   behind the answer.
3. **Key findings.** The load-bearing conclusions, each as: the finding, its
   **confidence tag**, and its **citation(s)**. Order by importance to the decision.
4. **Contested / uncertain.** Points where credible sources disagree: state both
   sides, the citations, and how you weighted them. Include your lean if you have one,
   labeled as a lean.
5. **What we could not determine.** The honest unknowns and, where useful, what it
   would take to resolve them.
6. **Risks / contrarian view.** What the contrarian lens surfaced: the strongest case
   against the bottom line, so the reader sees it rather than discovering it later.
7. **Sources.** The full citation list (title, source/author, date, URL), so every
   claim above is checkable.

For a quick check, sections 4 to 6 can compress to a line or two, but never disappear:
even a fast answer states its confidence and its main caveat.

## Confidence labeling in the report

- Put a tag on each key finding, not just an overall score. Readers act on individual
  claims, not on averages.
- When you resolved a contradiction, say so and why ("resolved in favor of the 2026
  primary source over the 2022 blog summary").
- When you did not, keep it in "contested," not smuggled into "key findings."

## What synthesis must never do

- **Never present unverified as verified** to make the narrative cleaner. If Step 4
  did not confirm it, it is not a key finding; it is contested or unknown.
- **Never drop the contrarian findings** because they complicate the story. They are
  the highest-value part of the report precisely because a naive pass would miss them.
- **Never invent a citation** to support a claim you believe but could not source. The
  claim is Unknown; label it and move on.
- **Never average away a real disagreement** into a false consensus.

## Handing off to a writer

If a separate writing or voice skill will produce the final prose (a post, a memo, a
brief), give it the structured findings above (claims + confidence + citations + the
contested/unknown lists), not a pre-written narrative. The writer owns tone; this
skill owns which claims are safe to make and how strongly. Keep the citations attached
through the handoff so the final piece stays traceable.
